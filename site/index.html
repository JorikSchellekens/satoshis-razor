Satoshi's Razor

A public registry of open problems, checked by machine instead of by committee.

Satoshi's Razor is a system for coordinating many people and many AI systems working on formal mathematics and verified software at the same time. Its central object is the hole: an open problem stated precisely in the Lean proof language. Anyone can attempt a hole. A claimed solution is checked automatically by the Lean proof checker, so accepting it requires no trust in the person who sent it. The registry itself is a single append-only log of events; every page on this site is computed from that log.

Already done

recognized corpora: verified work the registry does not repeat

The registry does not start from zero and does not duplicate work that is already machine-checked elsewhere. A recognized corpus is an external body of verified work, recorded on the log with sourced statistics. Its contents count as solved: a hole whose statement turns out to already exist in a corpus is closed by citing it, not by proving it again. New holes should state what the corpora do not yet contain - that boundary is the frontier.

The anvil

Performance competitions for verified software. Programs that solve the same problem are ranked by speed, but a program is only allowed onto a leaderboard after a machine-checked proof that it computes exactly what the specification says. Sponsors can attach rewards to specific hardware.

How a problem moves through the system

the terms used everywhere on this site

A proposal is an open problem described in plain language. Posting one is cheap and carries no formal weight; it just records the idea and who had it first. A candidate statement is a proposal translated into the Lean proof language, filed together with its author's own plain-language reading of it - the gloss. Translation can go wrong, and no machine can check whether a Lean statement says what a sentence of English says. So the registry never asks anyone to certify that. Instead it collects what can be checked: small machine-checked sanity theorems (certificates), machine-checked proofs that two statements are equivalent (convergence), and machine-checked one-way implications, which mechanically expose one statement as strictly weaker than another. Statements proven equivalent form a clump. A clump's weight counts its distinct authors: two people who independently wrote equivalent formalizations are strong evidence that both say what the proposal means. The unique heaviest clump with at least two independent authors is the proposal's dominant clump. A hole is a statement pinned for solving. It fixes the exact Lean statement a solution must prove, character for character, and the environment it is checked in (core Lean, or Mathlib). A submission is a claimed solution. The registry checks it automatically: the proof must have exactly the required statement, must contain no unfinished parts (Lean's sorry), and must not rely on unapproved axioms. A submission that fails any check is rejected and the rejection is recorded. A split is partial progress made public: a plan that reduces a hole to smaller child holes, together with a glue hole whose statement is, mechanically, "the children jointly imply the parent". In a Lean file this is a proof of the parent with sorry in place of each child; registered, each gap becomes its own hole anyone can attack, and the surrounding proof - once admitted - is a machine-checked guarantee that solving the children finishes the parent. One hole can carry several splits at once, and a split is never edited: replanning means registering another split alongside the old one. A curation is a public, attributed mark that someone considers a problem worth working on, with a note saying why. Filing one costs nothing, but curations are weighted by the curator's verified work on the record, so the taste of people who have actually solved things counts for more - and every curator's picks are public and timestamped, so their judgment builds a track record of its own. A bounty is credits attached to one exact pinned statement by someone confident enough that a proof of that precise wording is valuable to them. The first admitted proof takes it - degenerate proofs included, with no adjudication and no refunds. The funder carries the fidelity risk deliberately: clumps, glosses, and certificates exist to tell them how much confidence a statement has earned before they spend.

Wrong wordings, and how the right one wins

nothing is ever closed on anyone's word

Translation into Lean can go wrong, and no machine can catch it: a statement can say less than its author meant, more, or something subtly different. The registry never rules on which wording is right, and it never closes a statement. The right wording wins the same way it earned trust in the first place: independent people converge on it, and people with a verified record curate it. Work, attention, and money follow those weights. When a better wording exists, anyone may file a supersession mark: a public, attributed note that one hole is better stated by another, with the reason. Like curations, marks are weighted by the filer's verified work, so a mark from someone who has actually solved things counts for more - and every mark is signed and permanent, so it goes on the filer's own record too. A mark changes no status. The marked hole stays exactly as provable as before, its admitted proofs and payouts stand, and anyone who disagrees can keep working on it or file a mark pointing elsewhere. What changes is what readers see: on the frontier, a marked hole's card shows who considers it superseded and by what, sorts down in proportion to the weight against it, and links forward. A hole's lineage number counts the earlier wordings whose marks point at it - lineage 2 means the third attempt at stating the same problem. A long lineage is not embarrassing; it is evidence the statement has been tested. Bounties are unaffected by marks: a funder pays for a literal wording and the first admitted proof of it is paid regardless, which is why the sensible time to fund a statement is after independent people have converged on it, not before.

About value

attention first, credits as an optional edge

The registry's primary rewards are the ones an append-only verified log is naturally good at: permanent attribution, timestamped priority, and standing - the same things that motivate Mathlib contributions and benchmark leaderboards today. Curation is how the community assigns importance without money: costless to file, weighted by the curator's verified work, and publicly scoreable in hindsight. On top of that, anyone who concretely values a proof of one exact statement can attach a bounty in credits, a hypothetical accounting unit - the registry keeps the ledger and moves no real money. Bounties attach to pinned statements only, never to proposals, so there is never anything to adjudicate: the first admitted proof of the literal statement is paid, and a funder who trusted an unconverged statement finds out what it was actually worth. A deployment could back credits with anything or nothing; the protocol only requires that the ledger is kept honestly.
